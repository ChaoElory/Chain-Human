A B S T R A C T
Navigating the complexities and uncertainties of the modern business landscape requires resilient supply chains
to ensure long-term viability and success. Referring to Industry 5.0 principles, Supply Chain Resilience must
accommodate the characteristics of adaptability, sustainability, customer centricity, and innovativeness to
accommodate the ambidextrous characteristics. The present study aims to address its objectives by examining
current trends and themes in Supply Chain Ambidexterity (SCA) and exploring the intricate relationship among
Supply Chain Ambidexterity, Resilience, Agility, and Digital Transformation while also identifying possible di­
rections for future research. Through a bibliometric analysis of 290 articles from the Web of Science (WoS) and
Scopus databases until August 2024, complemented with a systematic literature review of 77 articles, this
research proposed an integrated research framework. It reveals a significant relationship between Digital
Transformation, Dynamic capabilities, and their role in enhancing SCA, with Digital Transformation as an
influencing factor. The research highlights the influence of SCA on resilience and agility empowered by Digital
Transformation. This research also proposes ten propositions based on the systematic literature review and
identifies research gaps for each proposition. This study is positioned at the nexus of bibliometric analysis and
systematic literature review to identify research trends and provide a s an overall comprehensive overview of
SCA in the context of Dynamic Capabilities, Digital Transformation, Supply Chain Resilience, and Agility. Its
findings present the current state of research in SCA and, therefore, guide future academic endeavors in the
dynamic arena. This research serves as a significant resource by pinpointing existing research gaps, delineating
future directions, and providing practical recommendations for implementation within the industry.
1. Introduction
Viewed by practitioners, the supply chain (SC) serves as a conduit for
material flow (unidirectional), cash flow (unidirectional), and critical
bidirectional information flow. Digital technology is pivotal in
enhancing this information flow, which is essential for resilient and agile
SC operations. An ambidextrous SC adeptly manages material and cash
flows while responding to changing conditions through adaptation and
recovery approaches. In the literature, balancing efficiency and flexi­
bility is likely vital for SC resilience during disasters (Birkinshaw and
Gupta, 2013; Gu et al., 2021). Ambidexterity, a multi-dimensional
concept (Gibson and Birkinshaw, 2004), entails a strategic fusion of
adaptability and alignment across internal SC departments and external
entities (Hald and Nordio, 2020). This approach allows SCs to innovate
while maintaining day-to-day functions (Guemes Castorena and Ruiz,
2017). Supply Chain Ambidexterity concurrently deploys capabilities
for alignment and adaptation (Aslam et al., 2020), vital for effective
supply chain management (Rojo et al., 2016).
An ambidextrous supply chain strategy forms the foundation of
business success and competitive capabilities (Kristal et al., 2010). In­
dustry 5.0, the successor to Industry 4.0, builds on the ideas of a
human-centric approach, sustainability, and resilience (Nayeri et al.,
2023), referring to the resilience of not only the manufacturing facilities
but also integrated supply networks as well (Ivanov, 2022). This
heightened emphasis on Supply chain resilience (SCR) is crucial within
the context of Industry 5.0 (Khan and Abonyi, 2022). Digital technolo­
gies and adaptive planning enhance disaster resilience and viability
through visibility, real-time monitoring, and data-driven decision-­
making processes (Belhadi et al., 2022; Tan, 2014). These technologies
enhance operational efficiency and strategic flexibility, enabling fast
response to disasters. SCA, enabled by digital technologies, effective
information flow, and adaptive planning, strengthens resilient

2. Literature review
2.1. Supply chain ambidexterity
Ambidexterity refers to the ability of an organization to simulta­
neously exploitation of existing capabilities and exploration of new
opportunities (Güemes Castorena and Ruiz-Monroy, 2020; Wang et al.,
2021), and it plays a crucial role in aligning systems and resources and
balancing with mutually exclusive processes and aims (Shams et al.,
2021). To achieve SCA, organizations must develop capabilities to
innovate and adapt (exploration) while focusing on efficiency and
optimization (exploitation). In the supply chain context, ambidexterity
allows companies to effectively manage operational efficiency and
strategic innovation in response to market changes. This balance be­
tween stability and flexibility leads to improved performance, increased
revenue, and enhanced market competitiveness (Suharto, 2023). This
Strategic management involves balancing and integrating exploratory
and exploitative activities to manage resources effectively in the short
and long term to achieve SCA (Rojo Gallego Burin et al., 2020).
SCA leverages an SC unit that underpins routine tasks and efficiency,
while secondary structures enable innovation and non-routine tasks
(Xiao et al., 2012). This is accomplished through the balancing of spe­
cific exploratory processes with exploitative tasks so that flexibility and
efficiency can be established (Gibson and Birkinshaw, 2004). Agile
short-term responses to market pressure and adaptable long-term pro­
ductivity gains require agility and are accompanied by resource utili­
zation along with innovative strategies to exploit disruptions (Aslam
et al., 2018). The concept of ambidexterity brings insight into the
complexity of a supply chain and strategy assessment (Turner et al.,
2018). As a Dynamic Capability, it enables asset reallocation to address
emerging challenges and opportunities (O’Reilly and Tushman, 2011).
2.2. Industry 5.0
The vision of Industry 5.0 (I5.0) is "human-robot co-working," where
robots/machines will handle monotonous and repetitive tasks while
humans concentrate on critical thinking tasks, activities requiring
creativity (Demir and Cicibas¸, 2019; Maddikunta et al., 2022). It is still a
futuristic agenda focusing on the soft behavior of humans and machines
and sustainable practices amidst Industry 4.0, hence not just on tech­
nological scope (Frederico, 2021). It enables real-time synchronization
of production for personalized products (Dwivedi et al., 2023), fosters
collaboration and automation (Mihardjo et al., 2019), aligns with sus­
tainable growth and environmental goals (Hein-Pensel et al., 2023;
Saniuk et al., 2022), and experience-driven manufacturing sector,
concentrated on enhancing customer experiences (George and George,
2020). I5.0 integrates AI into daily life to enhance human potential
(Skobelev and Borovik, 2017) and leverages extreme automation and
Big Data while ensuring security, cutting-edge technological policy, and
an innovative ecosystem (¨Ozdemir and Hekim, 2018). It eliminates re­
petitive tasks and non-productive activities, boosts productivity
(Nahavandi, 2019), and emphasizes sustainability and resilience to
shape future supply chains (Nul and Petridis, 2021). This paradigm
enables quick responses to disruptions, enhancing stability and market
position (Ahmed et al., 2023). Pre-adoption focuses on IoT, Blockchain,
and Big Data to lower costs, reduce operation time, and enhance effi­
ciency (Javaid and Haleem, 2020; Nayeri et al., 2023)).

2.4. Supply chain resilience
It is an operational characteristic that enables a damaged or broken
supply chain to recover and be more robust than before (Brusset and
Teller, 2017). Resilience is a characteristic that industrial production
needs to become more robust, better prepared to withstand in­
terruptions, and capable of providing and supporting essential infra­
structure during times of crisis (Nul and Petridis, 2021). SCR further
refers to the ability of a supply chain to restore and sustain its operations
in the face of disruptions or unexpected events (Gu et al., 2021). Belhadi
et al. (2022) explore disastrous SC challenges like the COVID-19
pandemic and define it as the ability of a to endure and recover from
disruptions while ensuring the continuation of essential functions and
meeting customer demands (Zavala-Alcívar et al., 2022). Sazvar et al.
(2021) recommended the first stage in the resiliency approach to the
vulnerable situation of the firms. The SC could be able to hold the
disruption and its effects (Sazvar et al., 2021). Siva Kumar and Anba­
nandam (2020) argue on the various perspectives regarding human
factors, situational learning, stakeholder relationships, organizational
culture, strategies, SC’s complexity, event/demand uncertainty, and
associated risks. Based on the above arguments and facts, building a
resilient SC lies in the capability of SC to anticipate, adapt, and recover
from disruptions while maintaining operational efficiency and main­
taining the continuity of cash, information flow, and material even in
disruption (Gu et al., 2021; Güemes-Castorena and Ruiz-Monroy, 2020:
JA.P. Barroso et al., 2010). Mari et al. (2014) asserted that achieving
sustainability goals helps minimize deviations in resilience objectives.
The future supply chain resiliency can be analyzed through proactive
and reactive approaches (Sazvar et al., 2021).
2.5. Supply chain agility
SCAg involves a company’s SC ability to quickly adapt and effec­
tively respond to changes in the marketplace environment and disrup­
tions in their supply chain (Agarwal et al., 2006; Yang, 2014; Michael J.
Braunscheidel, 2018) and a manufacturing company’s agility in
responding to customer needs and changing competitive conditions
(Mirghafoori et al., 2017). Additionally, It is described as the ability of
an organization’s SC to promptly and effectively respond to challenges
and changes in demand and supply within the supply chain (Swafford
et al., 2008; Gligor et al., 2013). These multifaceted definitions highlight
the need to change external factors, which is a common theme in the
definitions of agility in the manufacturing industry. The strategic infu­
sion of agility in the supply chain network is necessary to gain unique
competitive advantages (Piya et al., 2020). Moreover, SCAg enhances
the value chain and reduces systemic risks, ensuring that customers are
served consistently (Khorasani, 2018).
This literature review synthesizes knowledge on SCA, I5.0, SC5.0,
SCR, and SCAg. The review highlights SCA’s role in balancing explora­
tion (innovation) and exploitation (efficiency), emphasizing the need for
agility and resilience in modern supply chains to thrive in uncertain
environments. Industry 5.0’s transformative impact through human-
robot collaboration, sustainability, and advanced technology positions
it as key to future sustainability-driven supply chains. Besides extracting
aspects of resilience and agility, which are integral elements of SCA to
enable organizations to recover from any form of disruption while
ensuring efficiency in their operations, current trends, interconnected
themes, and areas for further research can thus be derived from
answering these key research questions that open a deeper analysis of
the role of SCA in steering the modern complexities of supply chains.
This study is important in improving SCA’s understanding of its impact
on future supply chain strategies regarding the change in digital and
technological transformations.

4.5.3. Supply chain resilience as thematic cluster 3
This cluster combines the two keywords of the same business sense:

The thematic cluster “Supply Chain Resilience” encapsulated building a
robust, adaptable supply chain that can navigate disruptions effectively.
The keyword integration in this cluster emphasizes the importance of
resilience in maintaining operational continuity and strategic advan­
tages in a dynamic business landscape. SCR is the ability of a supply
chain to endure and recover from disruptions. It involves preparedness,
effective response, and the capability to return to or enhance perfor­

advancement, globalization, specialized manufacturing, centralized
distribution, outsourcing, reduced supplier base, and volatile demand
patterns (Malindretos and Binioris, 2015).

able to withstand and recover from disruptions like a natural disaster,
economic downturn, or supplier failure (Bak et al., 2023; Dickens et al.,

disruption modeling, and route optimization with unforeseen con­
straints (Sharma et al., 2023). After a thorough review of the previous
literature, SCR can be defined as “Supply Chain Resilience is the capacity of
a supply chain system to endure and bounce back from disruptions or un­
foreseen events, enabling it to continue its operations effectively by incorpo­
rating strategies and practices that enhance adaptability, recovery, and
continuity while minimizing the impact of disturbances.”

(Sharp et al., 1999). Sharifi et al. (2006) have defined SCAg as the
capability that allows the network of a firm to deal with uncertain and
volatile situations. SCAg refers to an ability of a supply chain to adapt
fast and effectively to changes in the business environment, market, and
customer needs. The other perspective is that SCAg is the capacity of the
supply chain to respond rapidly to unexpected shifts or drastic changes

readiness of an organization to adjust to changes and disruptions that
occur in its supply chain (Ladeira et al., 2021). Based on the compre­
hensive literature review, we can define SCAg as follows: “Supply Chain
Agility is the capacity of a supply chain to swiftly and effectively respond to
changes in market conditions, customer demands, and unexpected disrup­
tions while maintaining operational flexibility.”

The rise of digitalization is promoting more companies to adopt IT-

Resilience.

6. Discussion
The present study explores the intricate relationship between SCA
and key research themes through a bibliometric analysis, SLR, and
thematic analysis of 290 research articles extracted from the Scopus and
WoS databases. The authors have applied various applications of bib­
liometric and thematic analyses to identify the author, country statistics,
sources, and keyword co-occurrence analysis. In this section, we answer
the formulated research questions. The present study also proposes a
framework (refer to Fig. 9) to represent the interconnection between
Supply Chain Ambidexterity and key research themes like dynamic
capability, digital transformation, supply chain resilience, and agility.
This study highlighted that the first publication emerged in 2007; since
then, the number of documents has grown due to rising academic in­
terest in the SCA domain. The trend of publications over the years shows
a steady increase, with a notable surge in the research activity field from
2017 to 2024. However, a significant shift occurred after 2017, wit­
nessing a rapid surge in publications, with 245 articles in the last six
years indicating an explosive increase. The leading journal in the study
area is “The International Journal of Production Economics,” with the
highest maximum number of publications (17), representing 5.86% of
the 290 papers with overall 1355 citations, whereas “International
Journal of Operations and Production Management” holds the second

involved a meticulous investigation employing bibliometric analysis for
Research Question 1 (RQ1), offering a structured and in-depth review of
the SCA landscape. While for RQ2, the combination of SLR with bib­

spanning the SCR, SCAg, Digital transformation, and Dynamic capa­

7.1. Limitation of the study
It is crucial to address the limitations of this study. The review
focused exclusively on two databases, WoS and Scopus, to extract arti­
cles, potentially leading to a certain degree of database selection bias.
The selection criteria deliberately focused on articles within the scope of
SCA across diverse disciplines, such as business economics, Computer
science, Operational research management, Engineering, Environ­
mental science, Science technology, and other relevant topics. Notably,
this study confined its scope to journals published in English. However,
some essential articles may have been published in languages other than
English. There is potential to study the different research streams that
can advance the SCA research. This study’s horizons can be broadened
by empirically investigating the proposed research framework. Future
research can expand by utilizing the additional search terms and
exploring the concept of SCA in conjunction with other related concepts,
such as supply chain resilience and ambidexterity. This study focused on
supply chain resilience criteria for solving ambidexterity problems in
SC, and other criteria such as efficiency, flexibility, information flow,
and digital integration can be included for further exploration in future
studies. In summation, the burgeoning interest exhibited by researchers
and academicians in SCA and SCR presents encouraging prospects for
grappling with the complex challenges currently confronting industries.
Addressing these challenges through comprehensive research and
practical implementation stands an opportunity that holds considerable
promise. However, the present study illuminates the dynamic and
captivating research area of supply chain ambidexterity.
