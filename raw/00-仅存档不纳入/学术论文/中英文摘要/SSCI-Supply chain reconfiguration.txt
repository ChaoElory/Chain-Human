AB Supply chain resilience and reconfiguration are emerging disciplines
   that allow supply chains to recover from events and quickly return to
   normal or equilibrium levels of operations. This paper aims to provide a
   systematic mapping review to classify studies on supply chain resilience
   and reconfiguration. In total, 286 studies published between January
   2009 and May 2019 were identified, of which 94 were selected for review.

   the selected articles; geographical areas; type of research; research
   methods; and supply chain resilience enablers and reconfiguration
   characteristics. The major significance of this study is to provide an
   assessment of the state of existing knowledge on supply chain resilience
   and reconfiguration and to suggest future research directions. To the
   best of the authors' knowledge, this is the first systematic mapping
   study conducted on supply chain resilience and reconfiguration
   literature.

TI Tariff Shocks and Global Supply Chains: Network Propagation, Logistics
   Resilience, and System Reconfiguration-A Systematic Review
SO IEEE ACCESS
VL 14
BP 91479
EP 91505
DI 10.1109/ACCESS.2026.3703535
DT Review
PD 2026
PY 2026
AB Tariffs have re-emerged as central policy instruments, yet their supply
   chain effects are often reduced to border-level price shocks. This
   systematic review synthesizes post-2018 evidence on how tariff shocks
   reshape global supply chains and logistics systems across pricing,
   sourcing and relocation, inventories and lead times, network
   propagation, relationship disruption, and export performance. Following
   a PRISMA-guided protocol, 88 peer-reviewed studies (2018-2026) were
   identified through Scopus and analyzed using bibliometric mapping and
   mechanism-centered thematic coding. Three synthesis-level findings
   emerge. First, exposure is frequently underestimated when measured at
   the border or final-product level, as tariff burdens accumulate through
   intermediate inputs and propagate across multi-tier production networks.
   Second, firms adjust gradually and combine multiple strategies,
   beginning with reversible actions such as rerouting, buffering, and
   limited diversification, and shifting to structural reconfiguration as
   policy credibility increases. Third, tariff-policy uncertainty acts as
   an independent transmission channel, delaying irreversible investments
   and favoring flexibility over rapid footprint redesign. Protective
   tariffs often underperform in multi-stage value chains because they
   amplify upstream costs, disrupt production hubs, and induce diversion
   rather than domestic upgrading. These outcomes show that tariffs
   function as system-level shocks that constrain supply chain optionality
   rather than isolated price wedges. These dynamics reveal how global
   logistics systems achieve resilience through buffering, rerouting, and
   network reconfiguration under trade war conditions. The review concludes
   with a research agenda prioritizing measurement of adjustment
   constraints, relationship re-matching dynamics, and empirically grounded
   operational and network models to support managerial decision-making and
   resilient logistics system design.

   Reconfiguration: An Enhanced Bibliometric-Systematic Literature Review

AB Global Value Chains (GVCs) have driven the worldwide dispersion of
   manufacturing but remain highly vulnerable to macro-level shocks,
   including financial crises, geopolitical tensions, and the COVID-19
   pandemic. These shocks expose manufacturing supply chains (MSCs) to
   systemic risks, but limited research has explored how GVC
   reconfiguration mediates their impact on manufacturing supply chain
   resilience (MSCR). To address this gap, this study conducts an enhanced
   bibliometric-systematic literature review (B-SLR) of 120 peer-reviewed
   articles. The findings reveal that macro-level shocks induce GVC
   reconfigurations along geographical, value, and governance dimensions,
   which in turn trigger MSCR through node- and link-level mechanisms. MSCR
   represents a manufacturer-centered capability that enables MSCs to
   preserve, realign, and enhance value amid shocks. Building on these
   insights, this research proposes a multi-tier strategy encompassing
   firm-level practices, inter-firm collaborations, and policy
   interventions. This study outlines three key contributions. First, at
   the theoretical level, it embeds MSCR within a GVC framework, clarifying
   how GVC reconfiguration mediates SCR under macro-level shocks. Second,
   at the methodological level, it ensures corpus completeness through
   snowballing and refines bibliometric mapping with multi-dimensional
   visualization. Third, at the managerial level, it provides actionable
   guidance for firms, industry alliances, and policymakers to align MSCR
   strategies with the dynamics of global production networks.

AB Supply chain disruptions pose significant economic stability and growth
   challenges, impacting industries globally. This study aims to
   systematically review the literature on the use of simulation tools in
   managing supply chain disruptions, focusing on the historical evolution,
   prevalent simulation methods, specific challenges addressed, and
   research gaps. A systematic literature review was conducted using the
   PRISMA method. An initial pool of 236 articles was identified, from
   which 213 publications were rigorously reviewed. This study analyzed
   these articles to map the academic landscape, identify key clusters, and
   explore the integration of digital advancements in enhancing supply
   chain resilience. The review identified the chronological development of
   research in this field, highlighting significant contributions and
   influential authors. It was found that various simulation methods,
   including discrete-event simulation, agent-based modeling, and system
   dynamics, are employed to address different aspects of supply chain
   disruptions. Two primary research frontiers emerged from the analysis:
   the strategic reconfiguration of supply chain networks to mitigate
   ripple effects and the swift implementation of countermeasures to
   contain disruptions. The findings suggest a need for future research
   focusing on dynamic analysis and control theory applications to
   understand and manage supply chain disruptions better. This study also
   notes the increasing interest and need to use digital technologies
   (digital twins, artificial intelligence, etc.) in future research. It
   underscores the necessity for continued research to develop resilient
   and sustainable supply chain infrastructures aligned with the United
   Nations' Sustainable Development Goals. The identified research gaps
   offer a roadmap for future scholarly exploration and practical
   implementation.

TI Can Digital Twin Technology Enhance Supply-Chain Resilience? A
   Systematic Literature Review
SO SUSTAINABILITY

PD FEB 28 2026

AB Digital twin technology (DTT) creates a virtual replica of a physical
   object, system, or process and uses real-time data to support
   monitoring, analysis, and control. Although DTT is increasingly
   discussed as a means to enhance supply-chain resilience, prior evidence
   is fragmented and lacks an integrated view across disruption stages.
   This study conducts a systematic literature review of 89 peer-reviewed
   articles on DTT and supply-chain resilience, applying relevance-based
   screening to retain studies with substantive theoretical and practical
   implications. The review indicates that DTT applications for resilience
   are emergent but gaining momentum, and that their contribution differs
   by resilience stage. Specifically, DTT capabilities support preparedness
   through enhanced visibility, risk sensing, and scenario testing;
   resistance through real-time monitoring, early warning, and evaluation
   of mitigation options; rebound through response coordination, recovery
   planning, and adaptive reconfiguration; and growth through
   post-disruption learning and network redesign. The synthesis also
   identifies key barriers to adoption, including data quality limitations,
   high implementation costs, shortages of specialised skills, and
   governance challenges, and suggests that integration with complementary
   digital technologies often enables more advanced functionality. Overall,
   the study provides a stage-based consolidation of DTT capabilities,
   benefits, and barriers to guide research and managerial deployment.

   The purpose is to explain how digital solutions are embedded in core and
   support processes, reshaping coordination mechanisms, governance
   structures, and process maturity across interorganizational settings. By
   systematically mapping Digital Innovation Solutions to specific supply
   chain processes and assessing their Readiness Levels, the study advances
   BPM research and provides practical guidance for managing

   corroborate readiness assessments, ensuring a process-oriented analysis
   grounded in Business Process Management. Findings - The findings
   identify 37 Industry 4.0-enabled Digital Innovation Solutions that
   address nine trends and six process-related needs across the Music
   Industry Supply Chain. These solutions are unevenly distributed across
   core and support processes and exhibit different levels of maturity.
   Eighteen solutions operate at a commercial level, thirteen at an
   experimental level, and six remain conceptual. The results show that
   digital innovation primarily manifests through process reconfiguration,
   influencing coordination mechanisms, governance structures, and process
   maturity rather than through isolated technological adoption. Variations
   in Readiness Levels reflect both technological maturity and
   organizational and institutional constraints across supply chain
   processes. Research limitations/implications - Future research could
   further explore how process governance mechanisms, organizational
   capabilities, and interorganizational coordination shape the successful

   maturity assessments, could deepen understanding of how digital
   solutions evolve from experimental to commercial stages. In addition,
   research examining institutional and regulatory dynamics, as well as
   strategies for aligning technological innovation with process
   governance, would enhance the practical applicability of Industry
   4.0-enabled digital transformation in the music industry and other
   cultural and creative sectors. Practical implications - The findings
   provide actionable guidance for managers, policymakers, and
   practitioners in the music industry by clarifying how Industry
   4.0-enabled Digital Innovation Solutions support process reconfiguration
   across the Music Industry Supply Chain. By linking digital solutions to
   specific processes and Readiness Levels, the study helps decision makers
   prioritize digital investments, anticipate coordination and governance
   challenges, and align technological initiatives with process maturity.
   The proposed framework supports strategic planning by identifying where
   digital solutions are commercially viable and where experimental or
   conceptual initiatives require further organizational, regulatory, or
   ecosystem-level development. Social implications - This study highlights
   how process-oriented digital transformation can influence social
   outcomes in the music industry. Industry 4.0-enabled Digital Innovation
   Solutions can enhance transparency in copyright management, improve fair
   remuneration for artists, and broaden access to music creation,
   education, and consumption. By supporting more inclusive, accountable,
   and participatory processes, digital solutions may reduce power
   asymmetries between creators, intermediaries, and audiences. In
   addition, the adoption of digitally mediated processes can expand
   cultural access and participation, particularly through virtual and
   hybrid music experiences, contributing to the social sustainability of
   creative and cultural industries. Originality/value - This study offers
   original value by advancing Business Process Management research into
   the context of cultural and creative industries, an area that remains
   underexplored in BPM literature. Rather than examining digital
   technologies as isolated artifacts, the study conceptualizes Industry
   4.0-enabled Digital Innovation Solutions as process-embedded
   capabilities that reshape coordination, governance, and maturity across
   an interorganizational supply chain. By systematically mapping digital
   solutions to specific supply chain processes and assessing their
   Readiness Levels, the study provides a novel process-oriented framework
   that extends BPM theory and supports informed decision making in
   complex, digitally mediated ecosystems.

AB Purpose This study quantifies how digital technologies enhance business
   process resilience across supply chain stages, addressing a critical gap
   in process management literature on technology-enabled continuous
   improvement. Design/methodology/approach A random-effects meta-analysis
   of 70 studies (218 effect sizes) maps digital technologies (sensing,
   analytics, collaboration) to three supply chain resilience process
   stages (preparedness, response and recovery), with moderator analysis of
   economic and cultural factors. Findings Digital technologies are
   positively associated with resilience (r = 0.331), preparedness (r =
   0.332) and response (r = 0.322) gain more than recovery (r = 0.273).
   Analytics tools yield largest benefits in early stages (r = 0.36);
   recovery processes rely more on sensing technologies (r = 0.299).
   Effects are stronger in developing economies (r = 0.350) than developed
   ones (r = 0.267) and diminish as digital maturity rises (r = -0.007).
   Notably, long-term orientation culture (r = 0.003) positively moderates
   technology effectiveness, demonstrating how cultural factors shape
   digital transformation trajectories in business processes. Practical
   implications Organizations should adopt stage-specific technology
   deployment strategies while balancing technological implementation with
   governance mechanisms. In digitally mature environments, process
   managers should prioritize institutional improvements over additional
   technology investments to overcome diminishing returns and achieve
   sustainable resilience. Originality/value Our analysis reveals digital
   technologies more effectively support the "risk perception-dynamic
   response" process loop than "post-disruption reconfiguration-adaptive
   learning" processes. The identified "diminishing digital dividend"
   phenomenon challenges assumptions that technology alone ensures process
   resilience.
